Resources

This page contains a list of all the courses I've taken at the University of Alberta. You may find the description for each of these courses in the University of Alberta course catalogue. For each course, I include a description of the course and tips for doing well. For select courses, I give a shoutout to professors who went above and beyond in teaching. Please keep in mind that the difficulty and review I provide is subjective to my personal experiences immediately after finishing each course; your mileage may vary.


For reference, my difficulty scale is out of 5 with the following descriptors:


  • 1: Minimal applications, minimal tricky concepts, minimal tricky questions.
  • 2: Some applications, minimal tricky concepts, occasional tricky question.
  • 3: Some applicatios, occasional tricky concepts, some tricky questions.
  • 4: Lots of application, some tricky concepts, some tricky questions.
  • 5: Lots of application, lots of tricky concepts, lots of tricky questions.

Year 4 (2021-2022)

Semester 1

Class Professor Textbook(s) Difficulty Review
Electrical Circuits I (ECE 202) Dr. Xihua Wang Irwin and Nelms, Basic Engineering Circuit Analysis, Wiley, 11th Ed. 3/5

ECE 202 is an extension of the circuits concepts you learned in high school. Other than to derive maximum average power and capacitor and inductor phasor relationships, there is minimal calculus in this course, i.e., ECE 202 is all about algebra and problem solving. Xihua was one of the best profs I had: good pace, efficient, tons of examples, good homework practice, and excellent summary sheets. The class average was 2.9/4.0.


You will start off by reviewing Kirchhoff's laws (KVL and KCL) then quickly build upon them with more powerful analysis techniques with DC circuits. The major techniques include loop analysis, mesh analysis, superposition, source transformation, Thevenin theorem, and Norton theorem. The later parts of the course introduces AC circuit analysis in the frequency domain; all of the DC techniques apply to the AC case. The course finishes with an introduction to complex power, magnetic coupling, and transformers.
Introduction to Digital Logic Design (ECE 210) Dr. Duncan Elliot Vahid and Lysecky, Introduction to Digital Logic Design. 2/5

Very interesting topics and extremely non-traditional teaching style with Duncan Elliot. Duncan provides so many bonus marks on his test that in my year 10+ students got over 100% on the final exam. The labs were quite fun, though get ready to self-learn the basics of VHDL because the course and lab section don't go at the same pace. Overall, other than occasionally super long lab assignments, this course was pretty low stress for high reward. The class average was 2.8/4.0.


The course introduces Boolean algebra, logic gates, Karnaugh maps, MUX/DEMUX, adders, subtractors, programmable logic devices, FPGAs, finite state machines, and everything else in a standard introduction to digital logic course.
Orientation to Cooperative Education (ENGG 299) Dustin Edwards n/a 1/5 If you're in co-op, you'll attend this course once per week. You need minimal effort to pass this course (this course is graded as CR/NC like your 100 level engineering professionalism courses), as the assignments are simply to create a resume and practice interviews with your peers. The rest of the class is just an introduction to what co-op is, the jobs you can take, the resources available to you, and job expectations.
Materials Science I (MAT E 201) Camila Santander Askeland and Wright, The Science and Engineering of Materials, 7th Ed. 2/5 This course was mostly review from CHEM 105 (intro chem 2) with very minor extensions. The vast majority of the questions in this class are plug and chug, so do a few practice questions and you'll be fine for the exam. If you're questioning why ECE students need to take this course, it's because of one thing: engineering accreditation requirements (just kidding haha... but maybe). If you take one thing away from this course, remember Miller indices because they come back in condensed matter physics (PHYS 415). The class average for this course was 2.8/4.0.
Theores of Functions of a Complex Variable (MATH 311) Dr. James D. Lewis n/a 2/5 Likely the most geometrically beautiful and easiest math class I've ever taken. Dr. Lewis was a gem who made math fun, had VERY flexible deadlines, and wore his world-famous suspenders to class each day. This course is the complex calculus analogue to real analysis. You will learn about complex functions, Cauchy's equation, residues, and more fun things. Many of the proofs in this course involve drawing pretty diagrams and epsilon-delta arguments. Lewis had open-book exams which focused mainly on computations, which made the class trivial to pass. The class GPA was 3.1/4.0.
Electricity and Magnetism (PHYS 281) Dr. Douglas Gingrich Young and Freedman, “University Physics”, 15th Ed. 3/5 Get ready to flex your vector calculus chops in this course! Scared? Well good, because I was just kidding... sorta ;). This course teaches Maxwell's equations (which require vector calculus) and many applications from his equations. By the powers of geometrical symmetry, you will simplify the vector calculus into single-variable calculus or even just algebraic equations. The course also goes on a tangent by introducing capacitors and basic circuit analysis; if you're taking ECE 202, this section will be a cakewalk as it is tailored for science students who don't take circuit analysis. Overall, the assignments and exams were quite difficult, so make sure you grind a ton of practice questions. The class GPA was 2.9/4.0.
Experimental Physics for Engineers (PHYS 292A) Dr. Juan Pablo Yanez Garza n/a 2/5 Engineering physics students unite! This will probably be your first course where you meet everyone in your engphys cohort. PHYS 292 is a very chill class that you take once a week, and it rotates between lecture and lab each week. The lectures will introduce you to the relevant physics concepts and statistical chops you need to complete the lab in the following week. You typically have one week to complete the lab report, but for the big formal lab report, you'll have two-to-three weeks to write up to 10 pages of "research." Be warned, you will likely need to do self-studying to learn how to use the statistical tests presented. You will also learn how to use Python to analyze data (though if you're lazy or are a masochist, you're allowed to use Excel for analysis later on). The coolest experiment in this course (in my humble opinion) is the superconductor lab, where you get to make a superconductor and hover a magnet over it when the superconductor is cooled with liquid nitrogen. Do well in this course as your course mark is determined at the end of PHYS 292B in the next semeseter, which takes into account your grades in PHYS 292A.

Semester 2

Class Professor Textbook(s) Difficulty Review
Engineering Thermodynamics (CH E 243) Dr. Ravin Narain Cengel, Boles, and Kanoglu, Thermodynamics: An Engineering Approach, 9th Ed., McGraw-Hill. 2/5

This class presented a straightforward approach to the first and second laws of thermodynamics. The majority of the problems in this course involved setting up energy and mass balance equations then plugging in values from thermodynamic tables. There are short assignments due each week, which are a great source of practice (and free marks). The class GPA for this course was a 2.7/4.0. No matter which prof you have for CH E 243, you will be in good hands.


The first half of the course presented fundamental tools for energy and mass analysis alongside applications to thermodynamic devices (e.g., throttler, diffuser, turbine). The second half of the course focused on the efficiency and entropy of these devices and thermodynamic cycles. This course covered the Otto, Diesel, Brayton, refrigeration, and Rankine cycles.
Electrical Circuits II (ECE 203) Dr. Gregory Kish, Anjana Wijesekera Irwin and Nelms, Basic Engineering Circuit Analysis, Wiley, 11th Ed. 2/5

Circuits II is a continuation of circuits I. All of the network theorems and analysis techniques (minus magnetic coupling and complex power) apply in this course, so make sure you know them by heart. In my opinion, the best way to do well in this course is to grind practice problems and sample exams. Top tips: know Norton, Thevenin, mesh, and loop analysis; make a nice summary sheet on bode plots and op-amps; do a bunch of practice questions; and you'll be golden. The class GPA for this course was 3.1/4.0.


The first part of this course focuses on analyzing first- and second-order circuits (i.e., using differential equations). Don't worry if you're rusty at solving ODEs—this course presents many shortcuts to solving them. The second part of this course analyzes circuits in the frequency domain. Here, we rely on phasors and eventually the Laplace transform. Bode plots are also introduced to analyze the frequency response of circuits. The third part of this course studies operational amplifiers and diodes, which play a key role in larger integrated circuits.
Continuous Time Signals and Systems (ECE 240) Dr. Horacio Marquez Marquez, Continuous-Time Signals and Systems, 2022. 2/5 This course is largely a review of Fourier series and Laplace transforms from MATH 201 (differential equations). At the start of the course, you cover fundamental concepts like linearity, superposition, BIBO stability, memory, convolution, and elementary signal properties. You then build on these concepts with linear time invariant (LTI) systems and analyze these systems using Fourier and Laplace. You then learn the relationship between the Fourier and Laplace transforms. You also touch on cool topics such as AM radio and filter design (filters are also covered in ECE 203). Overall, this was a very straightforward course where you prove various properties then use them to solve computational problems. If can do the assignments and practice exams, you will do well in the course. As for the prof, Horacio was pretty great at teaching and his exams were quite fair. The class GPA for this course was 3.1/4.0.
Classical Mechanics (PHYS 244) Dr. Kirk Kaminsky Taylor, Classical Mechanics. 4/5

If you liked Newtonian mechanics, you'll love this course! This was one of the most difficult classes I've taken, courtesy of Kirk, but also one of the most satisfying and beautiful courses I've had the pleasure of studying. Kirk is extremely passionate about teaching physics and has very high expectations for his students. He also programmed a physics simulator on the Apple App Store, which is frequently used in class to show some cool simulations. The class GPA for this course was 2.8/4.0.


The most difficult part about this course is setting up the equations and being extremely diligent with algrebra/calculus. Each assignment took multiple hours to complete, so beware. Nonetheless, you will prove interesting problems in the assignments, such as: (i) Is there a contradiction in Netwon's third law for two charges approaching each other from orthogonal angles?. (ii) What is the angular frequency of a spring on a rotating system? (iii) Why do planets orbit the sun such that the line between the planet and the sun sweeps out equal areas in equal times? (iv) How do we generate symmetry? To do well in this course, understand the fundamental theorems well, be careful with your computations, and practice, practice, practice!


The first half of this course reviews many concepts from first year physics but takes them one step further; you'll learn Newtonian mechanics in polar and cylindrical coordiantes, intrinsic coordinates, velocity-dependent forces (e.g., drag), linear and angular momentum, and energy in the context of vector calculus. Then, you will analyze oscillating systems: damped oscillations, coupled oscillators, and small oscillations. The second half of this course is where things get spicy. You first prove the Euler-Lagrange equations and other topics in calculus of variations (e.g., geodesic problems, Brachistochrone problem) then use the Euler-Lagrange equation in the context of Lagrangian mechanics. Lagrangian mechanics is a powerful extension of Newton's second law in scalar form, and you will be able to solve increasingly complex problems such as the double pendulum problem in an algorithmic manner. Lagrangian mechanics leads to one of the most beautiful theorems about symmetry and conserved quantities: Noether's theorem. Towards the end of the course, you will learn about two-body central force problems, an extension of Lagrangian mechanics, which plays a critical role in orbital mechanics and quantum mechanics. Finally, you will cover the basics of Hamiltonian mechanics.
Introduction to Modern Physics (PHYS 271) Dr. Juan Pablo Yanez Garza Tipler and Llewellyn, Modern Physics; University Physics Vol. 3; Feynamn Lectures Vol. I 3/5

Ever watched Intestellar or wondered what Einstein did? Well now you can learn all about it! This course was a whirlwind introduction to special relativity, quantum mechanics, nuclear physics, and particle physics. JP is a pretty chill and funny prof, so you're in good hands in this course (though I'm not a fan of the PowerPoint slides you learn from). The special relativity unit is arguably the wackiest, where you learn about time dilation, length contraction, and relativistic mechanics. You will also learn that E = mc2 is simply a special case of the more general energy-momentum relationship, E2 = m2c4 + p2c2 (this relationship will be derived by integrating the derivative of relativistic momentum with respect to position). After special relativity, you'll learn how light and charge became quantized and then delve into wave-particle duality alongside the Heisenberg uncertainty principle. You will then derive the Schrodinger equation and see how it leads to many crazy phenomena like quantum tunnelling and the EPR paradox. You will finish the course by deriving the quantum numbers (which form the basis for modern chemistry) and see the necessity for relativistic quantum mechanics. The class GPA for this course was 2.6/4.0.


Honestly, the best way to do well in this course is to take advantage of the extremely easy quizzes as the midterm and final will likely drag down your mark. For the special relativity unit, understand the meaning behind every equation, as plug and chug will not work here. For quantum mechanics, learn how to solve the Schrodinger equation and how to interpret and use the equations you derive from it. For most students, special relativity is a lot more difficult to understand than the quantum mechanics unit.
Experimental Physics for Engineers (PHYS 292B) Dr. Juan Pablo Yanez Garza n/a 2/5 PHYS 292B is the continuation of PHYS 292A from the previous semester. As with 292A, you will rotate between lab and lecture each week. At the end of this course, you will choose an experiment out of a list of prescribed experiments, write up a lab report, and present your findings in front of the class. Also similar to 292A, many of the statistical concepts taught in the lecture sessions are quite advanced for someone without having taken an introductory statistics course, but the application of the tests are quite straightforward. This course was overall pretty chill and a good opportunity to hang out with the engineering physics cohort (this class is only for engphys students). The class GPA for this course was 3.1/4.0.
